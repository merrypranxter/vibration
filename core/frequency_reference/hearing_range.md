## Infrasound: Below 20 Hz

**Definition:** Sound below the nominal lower threshold of human hearing.

Although generally inaudible as a pitch, infrasound is not imperceptible — it can be **felt** as pressure, unease, or vibration, especially at higher amplitudes.

### Schumann Resonances (7.83 Hz and harmonics)
Earth's electromagnetic resonance cavity (Earth surface to ionosphere) rings at:
- **7.83 Hz** (fundamental)
- **14.3 Hz** (2nd harmonic)
- **20.8 Hz** (3rd harmonic)
- **27.3 Hz** (4th harmonic)
- **33.8 Hz** (5th harmonic)

These frequencies coincide with human brain wave rhythms (theta/alpha), which has prompted extensive (if contested) research into biological entrainment effects.

### Earth Vibrations and Microseisms
- **0.001–0.1 Hz**: Earth's free oscillations after major earthquakes
- **0.05–0.3 Hz**: Ocean microseisms (double-frequency peak ~0.2 Hz)
- **0.1–1.0 Hz**: Building and bridge structural resonances

### Biological and Animal Infrasound
- **Elephant communication**: 14–35 Hz (low-frequency rumbles travel dozens of km)
- **Blue whale song**: 10–40 Hz (loudest biological sounds; 180+ dB, travels ocean basins)
- **Humpback whale**: 30–8,000 Hz (complex songs)
- **Pigeon infrasound navigation**: ~0.1 Hz (possibly used for long-distance orientation)

### Industrial and Artificial Infrasound
- **Wind turbine infrasound**: 0.5–5 Hz (subject of public health debate)
- **Organ pedal pipes**: 16–32 Hz (lowest organ notes felt physically)
- **Artillery and explosions**: 1–20 Hz (concussion waves)
- **Spacecraft launch**: 1–100 Hz (structural loads)

### Psychophysiological Effects
High-amplitude infrasound (~100+ dB) can cause:
- Disorientation, anxiety, unease
- Eye vibration (resonance near 18–19 Hz)
- Resonance in body cavities (lung, stomach: 4–8 Hz)
- Some researchers associate certain "haunted house" phenomena with 18.98 Hz infrasound

## Sub-Bass: 20–60 Hz

**Perception:** More **felt** than heard. Physical pressure on chest and body. The "weight" and "power" of sound.

**Wavelength:** 5.7 m (60 Hz) – 17.2 m (20 Hz) — larger than most rooms, creating room mode problems in acoustic design.

### Key Frequencies
| Frequency | Note | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 27.5 Hz | A0 | Lowest note on standard piano |
| 32.7 Hz | C1 | Lowest note of pipe organ pedal |
| 40 Hz | — | Gamma brainwave; 40 Hz therapeutic stimulation |
| 41.2 Hz | E1 | Low E string of bass guitar |
| 43.7 Hz | F1 | Low F of five-string bass |
| 55.0 Hz | A1 | Open A string of contrabass |

### Acoustic Engineering Notes
- **Room modes**: Sub-bass frequencies create standing waves in rooms; dimensions of 3–8 m create modes in this range
- **Speaker design**: Subwoofers specifically engineered for this range; require large cone area and/or enclosure volume
- **Music production**: Too much sub-bass on small speakers is inaudible; proper monitoring requires full-range speakers

### Instruments / Sources
- Bass guitar (fundamental range): 41–300 Hz
- Pipe organ pedal: 16–65 Hz
- Kick drum fundamental: 40–80 Hz
- Bass tuba: 29–350 Hz

## Bass: 60–250 Hz

**Perception:** Warmth, body, weight, depth. The foundation of music and voice.

**Wavelength:** 1.4 m (250 Hz) – 5.7 m (60 Hz)

### Psychoacoustics
This range provides the **fundamental energy** of bass instruments and the **warmth** in voices and string instruments. Too much boost causes **boomy, muddy** sound; cut causes **thin, weak** sound.

### Key Frequencies
| Frequency | Note | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 65.4 Hz | C2 | Low C of bass guitar/piano |
| 80 Hz | — | Bass guitar warmth region |
| 98.0 Hz | G2 | Open G of bass guitar |
| 110 Hz | A2 | Open A string of guitar/violin |
| 130.8 Hz | C3 | Low register tenor voice |
| 136.1 Hz | C#3 | OM frequency — Earth year |
| 146.8 Hz | D3 | Open D of standard guitar |
| 174 Hz | — | Solfeggio 1st tone |
| 196 Hz | G3 | Open G string of guitar |

### Room Acoustics
Bass frequencies couple strongly with room dimensions, creating **resonance modes** that colour the sound. Bass trapping (porous absorbers, membrane absorbers) is essential for accurate monitoring.

### Instruments / Sources
- Bass guitar: 41–300 Hz (fundamental)
- Cello: 65–1,000 Hz (fundamental range 65–698 Hz)
- Male voice (bass): 80–350 Hz
- Kick drum: 60–150 Hz (body and attack)
- Piano bass strings: 27.5–100 Hz

## Low Midrange: 250–500 Hz

**Perception:** Presence, fullness, "body" of voices and instruments. Can sound nasal or "boxy" if over-emphasised.

**Wavelength:** 0.69 m (500 Hz) – 1.37 m (250 Hz)

### Key Frequencies
| Frequency | Note | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 256 Hz | C4 (sci.) | Root chakra; scientific pitch C |
| 261.63 Hz | C4 | Middle C |
| 285 Hz | D4 (approx) | Solfeggio 2nd tone |
| 288 Hz | D4 (approx) | Sacral chakra |
| 320 Hz | E4 (approx) | Solar plexus chakra |
| 329.6 Hz | E4 | Top string of guitar (open E) |
| 341.3 Hz | F4 (approx) | Heart chakra |

### Equalization Guide
- **Boost at 250–300 Hz**: Adds warmth and fullness to thin-sounding instruments
- **Cut at 250–400 Hz**: Clears up "mud" in dense mixes; reduces boxy room sound
- **The "nasal" zone** (400–600 Hz): Boosting here makes voices sound nasal or honky

### Instruments / Sources
- Violin (fundamental): 196–3,136 Hz
- Male voice (fundamental): 85–300 Hz
- Snare drum (body): 150–400 Hz
- Acoustic guitar body resonance: 80–300 Hz

## Midrange: 500 Hz–2 kHz

**Perception:** The **most important range for human perception**. Contains the bulk of speech, vocal melody, and most instrument fundamentals. The "telephony band" — all speech intelligibility is contained here.

**Wavelength:** 0.17 m (2 kHz) – 0.69 m (500 Hz)

### Key Frequencies
| Frequency | Note | Context |
|---|---|---|
| 384 Hz | G4 (approx) | Throat chakra |
| 396 Hz | G4 (approx) | Solfeggio 3rd tone |
| 417 Hz | G#4 | Solfeggio 4th tone |
| 426.7 Hz | Ab4 | Third eye chakra |
| 432 Hz | A4 (Verdi) | Alternative concert pitch |
| 440 Hz | A4 | Concert pitch standard (ISO 16) |
| 480 Hz | B4 (approx) | Crown chakra |
| 500 Hz | — | Telephone bandwidth lower limit |
| 528 Hz | C5 (approx) | Solfeggio "miracle tone" |
| 1,000 Hz | — | Standard audio reference |

### Speech Intelligence
The speech intelligibility range is approximately **500–3,000 Hz**. Early telephone systems transmitted 300–3,400 Hz, demonstrating that voice comprehension needs only this band.

**Speech formants** (resonant peaks of the vocal tract):
- **F1** (500–1,000 Hz): Vowel height (open/close)
- **F2** (1,000–3,000 Hz): Vowel frontness/backness
- **F3** (2,000–3,500 Hz): Individual voice quality

### Instruments / Sources
- Human voice (all registers): 80–1,100 Hz
- Piano (middle range): 130–1,000 Hz
- Violin: 196–3,136 Hz
- Trumpet: 165–1,175 Hz

## Upper Midrange / Presence: 2 kHz–6 kHz

**Perception:** **Brightness, attack, definition, intelligibility.** The ear is most sensitive here. Small changes are highly noticeable. Harsh if over-driven.

**Wavelength:** 57 mm (6 kHz) – 172 mm (2 kHz)

### Fletcher-Munson Peak Sensitivity
The human ear reaches **maximum sensitivity** in the 2–5 kHz range due to the ear canal resonance (~3,500 Hz in adults). This means:
- 3,500 Hz sounds ~15 dB louder than expected from SPL alone
- Maximum pain sensitivity also occurs here
- Engineers are careful not to over-boost this region

### Key Frequencies
| Frequency | Context |
|---|---|
| 2,000 Hz | Speech consonant region; vocal presence |
| 2,500–3,500 Hz | "Singer's formant" — professional vocal projection |
| 3,000–5,000 Hz | Maximum ear sensitivity |
| 4,000 Hz | Attack of drums, clarity of strings |

### Equalization Guide
- **Boost at 2–4 kHz**: Adds bite, presence, cuts through a mix
- **Boost at 5–6 kHz**: Adds consonant clarity, definition
- **Cut at 2–4 kHz**: Reduces harshness, ear fatigue, harsh room sound
- **Over-boost at 3–5 kHz**: Causes listening fatigue rapidly

### Instruments / Sources
- Snare drum crack: 2,000–5,000 Hz
- Vocal consonants: 2,000–8,000 Hz
- Electric guitar midrange bite: 1,000–5,000 Hz

## Presence / Brilliance: 6 kHz–16 kHz

**Perception:** Air, sparkle, "air" on cymbals and voices. Provides the sense of space and detail. Loss of this range makes audio sound muffled or "underwater."

**Wavelength:** 21 mm (16 kHz) – 57 mm (6 kHz)

### Key Frequencies
| Frequency | Context |
|---|---|
| 6,000–8,000 Hz | Sibilance (S, T, Z consonants) |
| 8,000 Hz | Cymbal body; "crispness" |
| 10,000 Hz | Cymbal shimmer; acoustic "air" |
| 12,000–16,000 Hz | Tape hiss; very high harmonics; "air" |

### Sibilance (6–8 kHz)
The "S" and "Sh" sounds in speech concentrate here. Microphone placement and de-essing targets this range. Digital harshness and aliasing artefacts also appear here.

### Instruments / Sources
- Hi-hat: 3,000–18,000 Hz
- Cymbals: 2,000–20,000 Hz
- Violin bow on string noise: 5,000–15,000 Hz
- Acoustic guitar finger noise: 6,000–12,000 Hz

## Ultrasound: Above 20 kHz

**Definition:** Sound above the nominal upper limit of human hearing.

### Animal Ultrasound
| Species | Frequency Range | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Horseshoe bat | 70,000–120,000 Hz | Echolocation |
| Common bat | 20,000–100,000 Hz | Echolocation |
| Dolphin | 200–150,000 Hz | Echolocation, communication |
| Dog | 40–65,000 Hz | Hearing |
| Cat | 45–79,000 Hz | Hearing |
| Mouse | 1,000–100,000 Hz | Communication |

### Medical Ultrasound
Diagnostic imaging uses 1–20 MHz:
- **1–5 MHz**: Deep tissue imaging, obstetrics
- **5–12 MHz**: Superficial structures, musculoskeletal
- **15–50 MHz**: High-resolution skin imaging

Resolution = wavelength of sound in tissue (water-like, ~1,540 m/s):
- 5 MHz → λ = 0.31 mm spatial resolution
- 15 MHz → λ = 0.10 mm spatial resolution

### Industrial Ultrasound
- **Ultrasonic cleaning**: 20–40 kHz (cavitation bubbles clean surfaces)
- **Non-destructive testing**: 0.5–25 MHz (flaw detection in metals)
- **Ultrasonic welding**: 20–70 kHz (plastic bonding)
- **Ultrasonic distance sensors**: 40 kHz (parking sensors, robots)

### Piezoelectric Transducers
Ultrasound is generated and detected by **piezoelectric transducers** — materials (quartz, PZT) that convert electrical signals to mechanical vibration and vice versa.

## Age-Related Hearing Loss (Presbycusis)

Hearing sensitivity progressively declines with age, particularly in high frequencies:

| Age | Typical Upper Frequency Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Child (10 years) | ~20,000 Hz | Full range |
| Young adult (20 years) | ~18,000–20,000 Hz | Peak sensitivity |
| Adult (30 years) | ~16,000 Hz | Slight high-freq decline |
| Adult (40 years) | ~14,000 Hz | Noticeable loss above 12 kHz |
| Adult (50 years) | ~12,000 Hz | Common |
| Adult (60 years) | ~8,000–10,000 Hz | Significant loss |
| Adult (70+ years) | ~6,000–8,000 Hz | Presbycusis established |

**Note:** This is a general trend. Individual variation is large. Noise exposure history strongly influences hearing loss. The "mosquito tone" (17,400 Hz) is used as a teen deterrent precisely because most adults cannot hear it.

**The "17 kHz test":** Can you hear it?
- Younger than 25: probably yes
- 25–35: maybe
- Older than 40: unlikely

## Fletcher-Munson Equal Loudness Contours

The **Fletcher-Munson curves** (1933), standardised as **ISO 226:2003**, show the SPL required at each frequency to produce the same perceived loudness.

### Key Observations

1. **Maximum sensitivity at 3–4 kHz**: The ear canal resonance boosts this region by ~10–15 dB. At 60 phon, a 3,000 Hz tone requires ~54 dB SPL, while a 100 Hz tone requires ~67 dB SPL.

2. **Low-frequency roll-off**: Bass requires significantly more energy to sound equally loud. A 40 Hz tone requires ~30 dB more SPL than 3,500 Hz at low listening levels. This is why bass sounds weak on quiet playback.

3. **"Loudness" button**: Consumer amplifiers historically included a loudness compensation button that boosted bass and treble at low volumes to compensate for this effect.

4. **Equal loudness at high SPL**: Curves flatten at high levels (above 90 phons), explaining why loud music sounds "fuller" and more bass-heavy.

### Phon vs. dB SPL
- **dB SPL**: Physical measure of sound pressure (objective)
- **Phon**: Perceptual loudness level (equal to dB SPL at 1,000 Hz by definition)
- At 100 Hz: 70 dB SPL ≈ 60 phon (sounds like 60 dB SPL at 1 kHz)

### Sone Scale
A linear loudness scale where doubling sones = doubling perceived loudness:
```
Loudness (sones) = 2^((phon − 40)/10)
```
A sound of 1 sone is defined as 40 phons (40 dB SPL at 1 kHz).
